Overview

What happens if you measure all 256 elementary cellular automata?

The project started from a simple decision: do not cherry-pick a few famous rules, but run the entire 256-rule ECA family through one shared quantitative pipeline.

Why this is worth doing

Elementary cellular automata are minimal systems: a one-dimensional binary line, local updates from nearest neighbors, and only 256 possible rules. Even under that extreme simplicity they produce periodic regimes, chaotic behavior, and mobile localized patterns.

The point of Grav is not to collect pretty pictures. It is to build a reproducible map of rule space that makes different regimes numerically comparable.

What is measured

  • Mean binary entropy across trajectories.
  • Normalized sensitivity to one-bit perturbations.
  • A Lyapunov-like estimate of perturbation growth.
  • A coarse indicator for glider-like structure.

What this reveals

Once the 256 rules are embedded into a feature space, the repository can study the shape of that space directly. Some rules concentrate near low-entropy, low-sensitivity regimes; others collect in the high-entropy, highly sensitive region associated with chaotic dynamics; a third zone contains more structured but still active behavior.

This does not yet amount to a formal theorem of phase structure, but it does replace qualitative hand-waving with a measurable geometric picture.

What comes next

The broader aim is to connect metric structure with actual localized dynamics: domains, interfaces, defects, and particles. That is why the repository also contains rule-specific search tools, especially around Rule 69.
